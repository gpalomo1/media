The Four Roman Pastas

These pastas are the epitome of inexpensive, comfort cooking. Each uses slightly different techniques and ingredients and has its own place in the canon of Italian food.

Cacio e Pepe

Cacio e pepe” directly translates from Italian to “cheese and pepper,” which is a creamy sauce made with a generous helping of pecorino romano, black pepper, and extra-starchy pasta water. This dish does not traditionally use butter or cream in its original recipe, and only contains four ingredients: pasta, pasta water, ground black pepper, and Pecorino Romano.

Carbonara

Pasta alla carbonara is made with egg, cheese, and crispy pork belly. To make it, combine al dente pasta with raw egg yolks, grated cheese, and a little reserved pasta water to make a sauce that coats the hot pasta strands as it cooks. Cubes of guanciale, or cured pork jowl, add a crispy pop of flavor.

Gricia

Pasta alla Gricia is made with pasta, guanciale, Pecorino cheese, and black pepper. The dish starts with guanciale. Add black pepper to the rendered fat, ensuring a hint of peppery flavor throughout each bite. Next, add some starchy pasta to aid in the emulsification of the fat and pepper to make a glossy sauce that coats the pasta.

Amatriciana

This pasta dish features long noodles and a Roman tomato sauce complemented by hints of pork and cheese. Amatriciana is a tomato sauce made up of crushed, peeled tomatoes as well as guanciale (pork jowl or pork cheek), grated Pecorino Romano cheese, and onions.
